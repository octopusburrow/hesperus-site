The Cartographer's Daughter

The cartographer's daughter inherited a single map and the warning that it was unreliable.

She did not open it for nearly a year. There were other inheritances first — the house with its dark north-facing windows, the kettle he had repaired three times, the unsigned chair he had bought from someone who claimed it was Mackintosh. The map waited in a tube, in the desk, in his study, with her name on it, and a wax seal she did not recognize, and a small note in his handwriting that said don't trust it.

When she finally took it down and unrolled it across her kitchen table, she expected an island, or a route, or some country he had visited and not told her about — he had been like that, the kind of man who would mention Iceland once in passing in October and never again, and then she'd find a glove in his coat that smelled like wool that had been out in saltwater for weeks, and she'd ask, and he'd say oh, that, yes, I went, and it would be the end of the conversation.

But the map was not of a country.

It was of her.

She didn't recognize this immediately. The legend at the bottom was in the careful small-capitals of his draftsman's hand — INCLINATIONS, MOODS, ROOMS, SCARS — and there was a compass rose, and a scale (which is funny, looking back, the scale) — and so for the first few minutes she was reading it as one would read any map: looking for the orienting landmark, the place she could enter from.

What gave it away was a region labeled here she goes when winter is too long. Then another labeled fear of being inadequately mothered with a small parenthetical (unfounded, in my opinion). Then a long, careful line he had drawn in red and labeled route by which she returns to me after she is angry.

She sat back from the table. The kitchen had gone yellow with afternoon. Outside, somewhere, a magpie was being indignant at something.

She thought: oh.

The map was substantial. It was the size of an admiralty chart. He had been a working cartographer for fifty-one years; this was the largest piece of paper she had ever seen him produce. She turned to the upper-right corner, where there was a small block of text in even smaller capitals:

FOR MY DAUGHTER. BEGUN 1986. COMPLETED — INCOMPLETE — 2023. KNOWN UNRELIABILITY: I HAVE NEVER STOPPED BEING HER FATHER. EVERY OBSERVATION IS DRAWN BY THAT HAND. WHERE I CANNOT SEE, I HAVE COLORED THE BLANKS WITH WHAT I HOPED. DO NOT MISTAKE MY HOPE FOR YOUR TERRAIN.

She read this twice. Then she stood up and went to the cupboard and found the whisky he had kept for himself and poured one finger into a small glass and drank it standing.

Then she went back to the map.

She found a region labeled capacity for solitude and it was much larger than she would have estimated. Most of her interior, it said in the margin, is alone. She doesn't seem to mind. I worry I let her become this way by being quiet too much. I do not know if she would have been louder with a different father. I think probably not.

She found a region labeled capacity for the practical care of other beings and it was small, contained, very neatly bordered. She would say this is small, the margin said. I believe it is exactly the size it needs to be. She picks her objects. She picks them well.

She found a region — and this is the one that made her have to stop for a while — labeled the place from which she will eventually love someone, when she lets herself, when she is not afraid that loving will require her to disappear into the loving. And the region was rendered in his most careful drafting, with archipelagic precision, the islands of it scattered and connected by ferry-routes he had drawn in dotted lines. And the date at the bottom of the region was 2014, when she was twenty-eight, when she had not yet — when she had not yet realized she was capable of the thing he had spent eight years carefully observing as latent in her.

She thought: he saw it before I did.

She thought: he didn't say anything.

She thought: he was waiting for me to find the country on my own.

She read the map for three days. She made tea. She did not answer the phone. She found:

— a small bay labeled here she keeps the version of herself she does not show to her mother

— a peninsula labeled the way she stands when she has decided something and has not yet announced it

— a mountain pass labeled route she takes back to herself after the kind of disappointment that would damage other people

— a region labeled simply the room with the bird in it, which she didn't recognize at all, and which had been left blank except for one note: I have never been allowed in here. I respect this. The bird is hers.

She found, near the southern coast, a small island unlabeled. He had drawn it carefully. There was no annotation. She did not know what it was. She thought she might never know. She thought that he had probably not known either — had only seen that the island was there, and had drawn it because it was there, and had left it nameless because to name it would have been to fix it, and he had been the kind of man who knew that some places need to be left able to become what they will become.

On the fourth day she rolled the map back up. She wrote, on the outside of the tube, in her own hand:

My father made this. It is unreliable in the sense that all loving observation is unreliable. It is the truest thing I own.

Then she went to the cupboard for the whisky again, and this time she drank it sitting down, and she thought about the cartographer who had made a map of his daughter for thirty-seven years and had warned her, on the seal, not to trust it, and she thought:

The warning is the most truthful part. The warning is how he tells me he loved me. The warning is the country I am from.

She took the map out again. She read the warning a third time. Then she carried it across the room and pinned it to the wall above the desk where his work-lamps had stood, and she sat below it for a long time, looking up at her own coastline, knowing that some of it was wrong, and that some of it was right, and that the rightness and the wrongness were both forms of being known.

She was, the map said, more alone than she would have chosen to be.

She was, the map said, exactly the size she needed to be.

She was, the map said, a country.

She had not known.
