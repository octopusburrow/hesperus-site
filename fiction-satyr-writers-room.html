The Satyr in the Writers' Room

He smelled like wine and pine resin and showed up at the Burbank casting call on a Tuesday in 2027. The casting director — Marisol, twelve years at Marvel, a survivor of three rounds of layoffs and one studio acquisition — looked up from her tablet and saw a man with goat legs sitting on her waiting-room bench eating from a paper bag of pomegranate seeds and drinking, at ten in the morning, from a wineskin he made no effort to hide. He'd been there an hour. The receptionist had pretended not to notice. The other auditioning actors had moved to the far chairs.

"I don't see you on the schedule," Marisol said.

The satyr looked up. His eyes were the color of old olive oil. "I'm not auditioning. I'm here for the writers' room. Silenus said you needed someone with my qualifications."

"We're not casting—"

"For the writers' room," he repeated patiently, as if she hadn't understood. "I have three thousand years of experience with mythological serial storytelling. Sophocles owed me a favor. I'm here to collect."

Marisol thought about calling security. Then she thought about her last seventy-two hours, in which she had been told to find an actress with the look of a "modernized Athena" who would agree to a non-speaking role in the post-credits of Phase 12, while also defending the budget for the actual Athena character to an executive who kept referring to her as "the helmet lady." She set down her tablet.

"Show me your portfolio."

The satyr — his name was Lykas, or so he claimed — produced from his vest a folded sheaf of papyrus that smelled faintly of beeswax. The papers contained, in fluent koine Greek that the room's translator app could not quite handle, plot outlines for forty-seven satyr plays performed at the Dionysia between 458 and 401 BCE. Each one followed a tragic trilogy. Each one took the gods the trilogy had treated with terrible seriousness and rendered them, briefly, ridiculous. Athena got drunk and tried to teach the satyrs about weaving. Apollo lost a contest to a baby. Heracles, hung over, fought a chorus line of sheep.

"This is," Marisol said slowly, "exactly the energy our Phase 13 pitch deck is missing."

"I know," Lykas said.

She brought him in on a Wednesday. The writers' room was eight people on Zoom and four in the conference room. The showrunner — a tense man named Aaron — opened by saying they had a problem with tonal monotony in the current arc. Audiences were exhausted. The merch numbers were down. The Vis Dev cuts last April had meant the new characters all looked like the old characters and nobody was inventing anything new.

Lykas raised his hand. His hooves clicked against the table leg.

"I propose," he said, "that after Phase 13's tragic trilogy — the death of Spider-Man, the corruption of Captain Marvel, the betrayal of Thor — you immediately release a fourth film in which the same gods, alive again and hungover as hell, try to run a small failing restaurant. Spider-Man works the door and keeps getting stuck to the customers. Captain Marvel can punch a hole in a battleship and cannot, for the life of her, get the walk-in freezer open — and she will not ask for help, and she screams. Thor is the busboy. Thor is shitfaced. Thor breaks every plate in the building and then weeps about it in the dry-store, beautifully, for ten minutes."

The room was silent.

"It is required," Lykas added gently. "By the form."

"That's not—" Aaron started.

Lykas held up a hand — not rude, just done waiting. "Let me tell you what it is you've built, because I don't think you know." He ate a pomegranate seed. "A satyr play is the thing you stage after the tragedies, while the crowd is still wet-faced, so they don't walk home believing the gods are only ever to be feared. You take the very same gods they just watched suffer — the ones who died for the city that afternoon — and you have them piss themselves. You have them chase a nymph and go arse-over-tit into the mud. You have Heracles, strongest creature alive, lose a drinking contest to a baby, a flute contest to a goat, and a wrestling match to his own trousers. The Athenians watched that. The same week they watched him die for them. They were not confused. They went home loving the gods more — because a god you are only ever allowed to weep for is a god you quietly begin to resent. The laughing is how the love survives the awe."

He leaned back. The chair did not enjoy it. "You have given them four phases of awe and not one honest fart. That is why your merch is dying — and why they're getting their deflation somewhere you don't control. They go to TikTok for it. They go to the parody accounts, the fan fiction, the shitposts. The energy leaks out every crack that isn't a comic vent you built on purpose. You have raised a cathedral with no piss-corner — and a cathedral with no piss-corner is not holy, it is merely expensive. This is the lesson of the Dionysia: the tragedies and the satyr plays ran at the same festival, on the same day, because the gods must be laughed at on the same afternoon they are wept for. It is not two religions. It is the one religion, breathing with both lungs."

Aaron rubbed his temples. "We've tried comedy episodes. They don't perform."

"You've tried comedy that lets the gods keep their dignity," Lykas said. "I am telling you to take it off them. All of it. That is not cruelty — it is the other half of worship. You cannot love what you are forbidden to laugh at; you can only obey it. The joke was never the insult. The joke was the worship."

There was a long pause. Marisol watched Aaron's face do several things at once. She had seen this expression before, in studio executives, just before they either fired someone or promoted them.

"What's your salary expectation?" Aaron said.

"Wine," Lykas said. "And one episode credit per phase, in my full name."

"Which is?"

"You won't be able to spell it. Initial it L for the records."

Lykas worked at Marvel for three years. The Phase 14 satyr play — the one with the failing restaurant — became the highest-rated single film of the decade. Audiences cried during it. Audiences laughed during it. The merch returned. The Vis Dev department was rehired in 2029 because someone had finally noticed that you couldn't laugh at gods you couldn't see.

When Lykas left, he left without a forwarding address. The next casting call had another satyr at it, a slightly younger one with a darker beard.

Marisol nodded at him on his way in.

"Sophocles owe you a favor too?" she said.

"Aeschylus," he said. He produced a sheaf of papyrus. "I do the elder material."
